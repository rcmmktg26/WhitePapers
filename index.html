Part I
Oil in the Consumer Economy
Section 01
Executive Summary

Key Findings

Crude oil is embedded in the cost structure of virtually every sector of the U.S. economy, not merely as a transportation fuel, but as the foundational feedstock for petrochemicals, a critical agricultural input, and the energy source powering global supply chains. With Brent crude trading at $88.13 on Bloomberg as of March 23, 2026, and the Iran conflict threatening further disruption through the Strait of Hormuz, this white paper quantifies the share of final consumer prices attributable to crude oil across major product categories spanning six sectors, models the macroeconomic consequences at escalating price levels, and examines the hedging infrastructure that allows businesses to manage this risk.

Oil's share of final consumer prices ranges from approximately 3–7% for finished electronics and restaurant meals to over 50–58% for retail gasoline, with most intermediate goods falling in the 8–40% range. These are presented as ranges reflecting estimation uncertainty; a full derivation methodology is provided in Section 3.1. When prices escalate, the macroeconomic damage compounds, though nonlinearly. Using a declining marginal pass-through model (Section 10), our central estimate is that oil at $150/bbl would add approximately 1.7 percentage points to headline CPI (versus 2.2pp under a linear upper-bound model). Net GDP losses, accounting for the offsetting benefit to U.S. energy producers, would range from $260–$320 billion annually, and recession risk would be high based on historical patterns documented by Bloomberg, though significantly dependent on the duration of the shock.

The energy futures markets, which shattered records on March 6, 2026 with 8.3 million CME energy contracts traded in a single day, provide the mechanism through which airlines, trucking companies, refiners, utilities, farmers, and municipalities attempt to insulate themselves from this shock. Those that hedged before the crisis are temporarily insulated; those that didn't are passing costs directly to consumers. Hedging is not free insurance, it involves real costs and trade-offs detailed in Section 15.7, but for energy-intensive businesses, it is the primary tool available.

Section 02
Introduction and Geopolitical Context

The relationship between crude oil prices and the broader economy has been a subject of intense study since the 1970s oil shocks, yet the mechanisms through which oil prices transmit into consumer prices remain poorly understood by the general public and frequently underestimated by financial markets. As Bloomberg reported on March 23, 2026, crude oil at $88.13 per barrel reflects a market "singularly focused" on the Strait of Hormuz, the chokepoint through which approximately 20% of global oil supply transits.

The Forbes newsletter "Why The Iran War Poses Risks To AI" and Bloomberg's coverage of diesel price concerns ("Why Diesel Prices Are the Real Concern for the Economy") both underscore that the economic impact of oil extends far beyond the gasoline pump. Bloomberg's Odd Lots podcast has run multiple episodes documenting how the Iran war is squeezing American farmers, redrawing the natural gas map, and chewing through American missile stockpiles, each a vector through which oil prices ripple into consumer prices.

The Federal Reserve's March 18, 2026 FOMC statement and accompanying economic projections were released against this backdrop, with Chair Powell's March 21 remarks underscoring the Fed's vigilance regarding energy-driven inflation pass-through. Vice Chair Bowman's March 3 speech on "liquidity resiliency, financial stability, and the role of the Federal Reserve" and Governor Waller's February 23 speech on the economic outlook both addressed the challenge of conducting monetary policy amid energy supply shocks.

Approximately three-quarters of each barrel of crude oil is refined into transportation fuels that power virtually all mechanized movement of people and goods. The remaining quarter yields petrochemical products serving as feedstocks for plastics, fibers, rubber, fertilizers, pharmaceuticals, and thousands of other materials. This dual role gives crude oil an unparalleled reach across economic sectors, a reach this paper quantifies in granular detail.

Section 03
Methodology and Analytical Framework

The oil input cost shares presented in this paper are derived from a composite framework integrating data from the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA), International Energy Agency (IEA), Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) producer price indices, the Federal Reserve's economic research and DSGE modeling, Goldman Sachs oil-to-CPI elasticity estimates, Forbes economic impact analysis, Bloomberg market data and sector research, and Resilience.org systems analysis.

3.1 Oil Cost Share Derivation Methodology

The oil input cost shares are derived using a three-layer decomposition for each product category. Layer 1 captures direct fuel input: the diesel, gasoline, or other petroleum fuel consumed in producing and transporting the product, calculated from industry-average fuel consumption data (EIA, BLS Producer Price Indices) and current fuel prices (Bloomberg, 3/23/2026). Layer 2 captures petrochemical feedstock: the petroleum-derived material content (plastics, synthetic fibers, chemical inputs), estimated from IEA petrochemical flow data and industry bill-of-materials analyses. Layer 3 captures embedded transport and energy: the oil cost embedded in upstream supply chain stages, e.g., the diesel consumed transporting fertilizer to a farm, or the fuel cost in delivering raw resin to a packaging plant, estimated from BLS input-output tables and EIA supply chain energy audits.

For each product, the total oil share equals the sum of these three layers divided by the average retail or wholesale price. Estimates are presented as ranges reflecting data availability and estimation uncertainty. Products where direct EIA or BLS data are available (gasoline, diesel, commodity plastics) carry higher confidence than products requiring composite estimation across multiple layers (cosmetics, restaurant meals). A full derivation workbook is available from the authors on request.

3.2 Macroeconomic Modeling

For macroeconomic modeling, the paper employs two complementary approaches for inflation pass-through. The Goldman Sachs widely-cited estimate that a sustained 10% oil price rise adds approximately 0.28 percentage points to headline CPI (via Seeking Alpha) is used as the starting-point marginal elasticity. However, recognizing that elasticities estimated for moderate price movements cannot be reliably extrapolated linearly to extreme price levels (see Section 10 for the nonlinear adjustment), we apply a declining marginal pass-through schedule at higher price levels to produce a central estimate, while retaining the linear extrapolation as an upper bound. The Federal Reserve's DSGE model produces a more conservative estimate: roughly +0.15 percentage points to headline PCE inflation for a 10% oil price increase, reflecting the Fed's emphasis on the narrower core transmission mechanism.

The GDP impact uses Forbes-cited estimates that each $10/barrel oil price increase reduces GDP by approximately 0.2% (roughly $35–50 billion per year), adjusted for the domestic producer offset described in Section 11.1. Recession risk assessments draw on Bloomberg's historical analysis showing that oil price increases of approximately 50% above trend have preceded U.S. recessions, presented as qualitative risk assessments rather than precise probabilities (see Section 12).

The pass-through elasticity for sector-level analysis uses sector-specific coefficients ranging from 0.30 (high-margin branded consumer products) to 0.90 (commodity products with transparent pricing), replacing the uniform assumption used in earlier drafts. See Section 13 for the full schedule and rationale.

Note on Estimates

Actual outcomes will vary based on duration of price shock, monetary policy response, substitution effects, demand destruction dynamics, and the supply-side response detailed in Section 12.2. Section 04
Transportation Fuels and Global Mobility

Transportation represents the most direct channel through which crude oil prices reach consumers. As Bloomberg's coverage highlighted, "Why Diesel Prices Are the Real Concern for the Economy", the impact extends far beyond the gasoline pump to encompass the freight, aviation, and marine shipping networks that move every physical product in the economy.

4.1 Gasoline and Personal Mobility

Crude oil costs typically constitute 50–58% of the retail gasoline price (high confidence, direct EIA data), with the remainder split among refining (approximately 14%), distribution and marketing (approximately 12%), and federal/state taxes (approximately 14–19%). Bloomberg reported Trump discussing "really good" talks with Iran partly to calm markets, markets where gasoline futures respond within hours to geopolitical developments.

4.2 Diesel and Freight Logistics

Diesel powers the freight backbone: long-haul trucks, delivery vans, locomotives, and shipping. Bloomberg's dedicated reporting on diesel as "the real concern for the economy" reflects that diesel price increases cascade into the cost of every product that moves by truck, train, or ship, which is essentially the entire retail economy. A single cross-country truck load burns approximately 400 gallons of diesel; at even a $1/gallon price increase, that adds $400 per trip, a cost ultimately borne by consumers.

4.3 Jet Fuel and Aviation

Airlines face jet fuel as 20–30% of operating costs, with a single carrier consuming billions of gallons per year. Forbes reported LaGuardia crash disruptions alongside ongoing aviation cost pressures, while Bloomberg noted airlines with pre-crisis hedges are significantly better positioned, a divergence that will manifest as fare increases, route cuts, and profitability differences across carriers.

4.4 Marine Fuels and International Trade

Bloomberg's Vitol coverage, "Oil Is Market 'Singularly Focused' on Hormuz", underscores that the Strait of Hormuz chokepoint affects not only crude oil supply but the marine fuel costs embedded in all seaborne trade. Approximately 90% of world trade moves by ship; a sustained increase in bunker fuel costs reprices the entire global supply chain.

Section 05
Petrochemicals, Plastics, and Manufactured Goods

Crude oil serves as the foundational feedstock for the petrochemical industry, producing the building blocks of plastics, synthetic fibers, rubber, solvents, fertilizers, pharmaceuticals, and thousands of other materials. The IEA has identified petrochemicals as the fastest-growing source of global oil demand.

The petrochemical value chain begins when crude oil is refined and a fraction, primarily naphtha and ethane, is "cracked" to yield ethylene, propylene, butadiene, and benzene. These are polymerized into plastics (PE, PP, PVC, polystyrene), fibers (polyester, nylon), rubber (SBR), and specialty chemicals for detergents, paints, adhesives, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals.

Part II
Macroeconomic Impact — Inflation, GDP, and Recession Risk
Section 10
Inflation Transmission: CPI and PCE Effects

Higher oil prices pass through quickly to consumer inflation through both direct channels (gasoline, heating fuel) and indirect channels (transportation costs embedded in all goods, petrochemical feedstock costs, energy-intensive manufacturing). Two authoritative estimates bracket the magnitude of this pass-through.

Goldman Sachs estimates (via Seeking Alpha) that a sustained 10% oil price rise adds approximately 0.28 percentage points to headline CPI, a widely-cited elasticity that captures both direct and indirect channels. The Federal Reserve's own DSGE model produces a more conservative estimate: roughly +0.15 percentage points to headline PCE inflation for a 10% oil price increase, reflecting the Fed's emphasis on the narrower core transmission mechanism. The March 18, 2026 FOMC statement and economic projections were formulated with these dynamics explicitly in view.

10.1 Nonlinear Pass-Through Adjustment

The Goldman Sachs elasticity was estimated for moderate price movements within historical trading ranges. Academic literature (Hamilton, 2003; Kilian, 2009; Baumeister & Kilian, 2016) consistently finds that pass-through rates decline at extreme price levels due to demand destruction (consumers reduce driving, defer purchases), substitution effects (businesses switch to alternative inputs), margin absorption (retailers absorb cost increases rather than lose customers), and contractionary policy response (Fed tightening reduces aggregate demand, limiting firms' pricing power).

We apply a declining pass-through schedule that reduces the marginal CPI elasticity by approximately 15% for each successive $25 increment above baseline, reflecting these well-documented nonlinearities. The linear extrapolation is retained as an upper bound.

Key Finding

At the current Bloomberg price of $88.13/bbl (roughly 17% above a $75 baseline), the Goldman Sachs model implies approximately +0.5pp added to CPI. If the Iran conflict drives crude to $125, CPI would absorb approximately 1.3–1.6 additional percentage points (central to upper bound), significantly complicating the Federal Reserve's inflation mandate as expressed in the March 18 FOMC statement.

Section 11
GDP Impact and Economic Cost

Higher oil prices subtract from GDP through reduced consumer spending power, higher business input costs, compressed margins, and the contractionary monetary policy response that energy-driven inflation may compel. Forbes-cited research estimates that each $10/barrel oil price increase reduces GDP by approximately 0.2%, or roughly $35–50 billion per year in the current economy, a rule-of-thumb broadly consistent with academic estimates (Hamilton, 2003) and historical experience.

Bloomberg's reporting on "China's Record Deflation Finds Dangerous Cure in Oil Shock" and Israel's disclosure that its economy lost over $57 billion during two years of conflict both illustrate the GDP destruction that energy and geopolitical shocks produce, effects now playing out globally as the Iran conflict enters its third week.

11.1 The Net Producer Offset

Why the U.S. GDP Impact Is Smaller Than It Appears

A critical nuance is that the United States has been a net exporter of petroleum products since approximately 2019 and a net total energy exporter since 2020 (EIA). This fundamentally changes the macroeconomic calculus compared to the 1970s and 1980s, when the U.S. was heavily import-dependent.

Higher oil prices generate significant offsetting economic benefits within the U.S.: increased revenue for domestic producers (Permian Basin, Bakken, Eagle Ford), accelerated drilling capital expenditure, job creation in energy-producing states, and higher federal and state royalty revenues. Academic estimates (Kilian, 2009; Herrera & Pesavento, 2009) suggest the net GDP drag for a net energy exporter may be 30–50% lower than the gross consumer-side impact, as producer-side gains partially offset consumer-side losses.

The net GDP impact remains negative because the consumer-side drag (affecting all 330 million Americans) outweighs the producer-side benefit (concentrated in energy-sector workers and shareholders). However, the net effect is materially smaller than gross figures suggest. Distributional effects are also important: energy-producing regions may experience boom conditions while energy-consuming regions experience cost-driven contraction.

Section 12
Recession Risk, Duration, and Structural Context

12.1 Historical Pattern and Risk Assessment

Bloomberg's historical analysis documents that oil price increases of approximately 50% or more above trend have preceded U.S. recessions in 1973, 1979, 1990, and 2008, a four-for-four record that makes this threshold analytically significant. However, each recession involved significant co-factors: the 1973 recession coincided with the collapse of Bretton Woods and price controls; 1979 with aggressive Fed tightening under Volcker; 1990 with the S&L crisis; and 2008 with the subprime mortgage collapse. Oil was a contributing cause in each case but not the sole cause.

Rather than assign precise probabilities, which would require a calibrated model beyond the scope of this paper, we assess recession risk qualitatively against historical precedent:

12.2 Supply-Side Response and Price Self-Correction

The scenario analysis in this paper holds supply constant, modeling only the demand-side consequences of price increases. In reality, higher prices trigger significant supply responses that historically moderate and reverse oil price spikes.

U.S. shale production is highly price-responsive. At $80+ WTI, Permian Basin operators accelerate drilling programs. At $100+, DUC (drilled-but-uncompleted) wells are brought online within weeks, and new drilling can add meaningful production within 4–6 months. The EIA estimates that U.S. tight oil production can increase by 500,000–1,000,000 barrels per day within 12 months of sustained $100+ pricing. OPEC spare capacity, estimated at 3–5 million barrels per day as of early 2026, concentrated in Saudi Arabia and the UAE, could be deployed to offset supply disruptions. Strategic petroleum reserves, Bloomberg reported that Energy Secretary Wright confirmed SPR releases began flowing on March 21, 2026, provide an additional emergency buffer.

These supply responses mean that the $125 and $150 scenarios are more likely to be transitory spikes (weeks to months) than sustained plateaus (years). The most probable path for a Hormuz disruption scenario is a sharp spike followed by partial retracement as supply responses materialize, rather than a permanent step-change. This does not eliminate the risk, a severe disruption could overwhelm supply responses for months, and even temporary spikes cause real economic damage, but it means the "sustained $150" scenario should be understood as a tail risk, not a central case.

12.3 Duration Matters: Transitory Spikes vs. Sustained Plateaus

The macroeconomic impact of an oil price shock depends critically on its duration. A three-month spike to $125/bbl has vastly different consequences than two years of sustained $125 oil.

Historical precedent is instructive: the 1990 Gulf War spike (oil roughly doubled) lasted approximately 4 months before retracing; the 2008 spike ($70 to $147) lasted approximately 7 months before collapsing; the 2011–2014 plateau ($90–$110) lasted roughly 3 years but involved a gradual rise rather than a shock. 12.4 Declining Oil Intensity

Why the Economy Is Less Vulnerable Than Historical Precedent Suggests

The U.S. economy consumes significantly less oil per dollar of GDP than during previous oil shock episodes. Oil intensity (barrels consumed per million dollars of real GDP) has fallen by approximately 50% since 1973 and by roughly 25% since 2000 (EIA data). This structural decline reflects vehicle fleet fuel efficiency improvements (CAFE standards), growth of the service and digital economy relative to manufacturing, electrification of heating, increasing renewable electricity generation (now approximately 22% of U.S. power), and a growing EV fleet (approximately 9% of new vehicle sales in 2025).

This means that a given oil price increase produces a smaller GDP drag and CPI impact today than the same price increase would have produced in 1979 or even 2008. The Goldman Sachs CPI elasticity already partially reflects this reduced intensity (it is a contemporary estimate), but the historical recession analogies in Section 12.1, which reference the 1973, 1979, and 2008 episodes, may overstate vulnerability by drawing on an era of higher oil dependence.

⚠ Current Risk Level

At the March 23, 2026 Bloomberg price of $88.13/bbl, the economy is approximately 17% above the $75 baseline, within the "low-moderate risk" zone. Bloomberg reports that markets remain "singularly focused" on Hormuz, and Trump's negotiation window with Iran creates binary risk: successful de-escalation could return prices toward $75, while failure or escalation could push prices rapidly through $100 toward $125+, approaching the historical recession-trigger threshold. However, supply-side responses (Section 12.2), declining oil intensity (Section 12.4), and the U.S. net producer offset (Section 11.1) all provide structural buffers that did not exist in earlier oil shock episodes. A rise in crude oil prices increases diesel fuel costs, which raises the price of transporting nitrogen fertilizer from plant to farm. Higher fertilizer delivered cost, combined with higher farm diesel, increases corn production cost. Higher corn prices raise animal feed costs, increasing beef, poultry, and dairy production costs. Higher livestock costs, combined with higher refrigerated transport, increase wholesale meat prices. Consumers facing higher groceries may demand higher wages, raising labor costs for restaurants, processors, and retailers, creating a secondary price wave.

Quantifying this amplification precisely would require a full input-output model beyond the scope of this paper. Academic research on production network effects (Acemoglu et al., 2012, "The Network Origins of Aggregate Fluctuations," Econometrica; Baqaee & Farhi, 2019, "The Macroeconomic Impact of Microeconomic Shocks," Econometrica) demonstrates that supply chain network effects amplify first-round commodity price shocks, with the magnitude depending on the commodity's network centrality and the number of production stages. Petroleum is unusually central in the production network due to its dual role as energy source and feedstock. The first-order estimates in Table 1 likely understate total consumer price impact by some margin due to these cascading effects, but we do not assign a specific amplification factor given the absence of a calibrated model for the current shock.

Bloomberg's "Odd Lots: The Chokepoint to End All Chokepoints" newsletter captures this systemic risk precisely, the Strait of Hormuz is not merely an oil chokepoint but a chokepoint for the entire global economic system built on petroleum flows.

How the Global Economy Hedges Oil Risk

As the preceding analysis makes clear, oil is embedded in virtually every product and service in the modern economy. That pervasive exposure creates enormous financial risk for every business that produces, transports, refines, and consumes energy, and for every industry downstream of it. Energy futures markets exist precisely to manage that risk. They are the mechanism through which airlines, trucking companies, refiners, utilities, farmers, manufacturers, and municipalities attempt to insulate themselves and their customers from the kind of price shock the Iran war has now delivered.

The scale of this hedging infrastructure is immense. Hundreds of distinct energy futures and options contracts trade across the world's major exchanges, CME Group (which owns NYMEX), Intercontinental Exchange (ICE), the Gulf Mercantile Exchange (GME), the Shanghai International Energy Exchange (INE), ICE Futures Abu Dhabi (IFAD), and the Tokyo Commodity Exchange (TOCOM), covering crude oil benchmarks (WTI, Brent, Oman, Murban, Shanghai SC), refined products (gasoline, diesel, gasoil, jet fuel, ethanol), natural gas (Henry Hub, Dutch TTF, UK NBP, Asian JKM LNG, and 30+ U.S. regional basis hubs), electric power (PJM, ERCOT, NYISO, ISO-NE, and European markets), coal, petrochemicals, NGLs, carbon emissions allowances, and a growing suite of energy transition products.

Section 15
Hedging Use Cases Across the Economy

15.1 Airlines

Airlines are among the most visible energy hedgers. Jet fuel typically represents 20–30% of operating costs, and a single carrier may consume billions of gallons per year. By locking in fuel prices months or years in advance through futures and options, airlines can stabilize ticket prices and protect margins. Southwest Airlines famously saved billions through aggressive fuel hedging during previous oil spikes. In the current crisis, airlines with hedges in place entering 2026 are significantly better positioned than those that were unhedged, a divergence that will manifest in fare increases, route cuts, and profitability over coming quarters.

15.2 Trucking and Freight

With diesel the single largest variable cost in long-haul trucking, and a single cross-country load burning 400 gallons, even a $1/gallon increase adds $400 per trip. Large carriers hedge diesel exposure using NYMEX ultra-low sulfur diesel (ULSD) futures and heating oil contracts, often supplemented by fuel surcharge mechanisms that pass residual risk to shippers. Smaller operators who cannot access futures markets are the most exposed and the first to exit the industry when prices spike, tightening capacity and amplifying the inflationary spiral.

15.3 Refiners and Crack Spreads

Refiners use crude oil and refined product futures simultaneously to lock in "crack spreads", the margin between input cost (crude) and output prices (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel). In the current environment, crack spreads have widened dramatically as refined product prices have outpaced crude, rewarding refiners who locked in processing margins while creating windfall volatility for those who didn't.

15.4 Utilities and Power Generators

Utilities hedge natural gas and coal exposure to stabilize electricity rates for consumers. A municipal utility that locked in Henry Hub natural gas at $3.00/MMBtu before the crisis is now shielding ratepayers from sharply higher spot prices. Conversely, utilities buying spot or with hedges rolling off are passing through higher energy costs to residential and commercial customers, a dynamic playing out across PJM, ERCOT, and European power markets simultaneously.

15.5 Agriculture

Agriculture is deeply exposed through both fuel and fertilizer. Natural gas is the primary feedstock for ammonia-based fertilizers, meaning fertilizer prices move in near-lockstep with energy markets. Farmers and agricultural cooperatives use futures to hedge diesel, natural gas exposure embedded in fertilizer costs, and transportation costs. Bloomberg's Odd Lots documented how the current energy shock is expected to drive fertilizer costs significantly higher for the 2026–2027 growing season.

15.6 Municipalities and Waste Haulers

Garbage trucks averaging 3–4 MPG and running stop-and-start routes all day are among the most fuel-intensive vehicles on the road. Larger waste management companies hedge diesel costs; smaller haulers and municipal operations often cannot, meaning costs flow directly to taxpayers through higher collection fees or property tax assessments.

15.7 The Costs and Risks of Hedging: What You're Trading Off

Futures and options provide powerful tools for managing energy price risk, but they are not free insurance. Any business considering a hedging program should understand the following trade-offs.

Premium cost: Options strategies (caps, collars, put spreads) require upfront premium payments that represent a real cost even if the hedge is never triggered. A typical crude oil call option at $100/bbl strike for 6 months forward might cost $3–$6/bbl depending on volatility, a meaningful expense that must be weighed against the protection provided.

Opportunity cost: Fixed-price hedges (swaps, futures) lock in a price. If oil falls below the hedged price, the hedger pays above-market rates while unhedged competitors enjoy lower costs. Southwest Airlines' famous hedging gains in 2008 were preceded by periods where their hedges were underwater. Hedging smooths costs, it does not guarantee the lowest cost.

Margin and liquidity requirements: Exchange-traded futures require initial margin (typically 5–10% of notional value) and daily mark-to-market margin. In volatile markets, margin calls can create severe cash flow strain, particularly for smaller companies. The March 2026 volatility surge generated margin calls that forced some participants to liquidate positions at unfavorable prices.

Basis risk: Standard exchange-traded contracts (WTI crude, NYMEX ULSD) may not perfectly match a company's actual exposure (Gulf Coast jet fuel, Midwest diesel, Appalachian natural gas). This "basis risk" means the hedge may not fully offset the cost increase experienced in practice.

Complexity and expertise: Effective hedging requires ongoing monitoring, position management, and expertise in derivatives markets. Poorly structured hedges can increase risk rather than reduce it.

These trade-offs do not argue against hedging, for most energy-intensive businesses facing the current geopolitical environment, some form of price risk management is prudent. But they argue for hedging with clear eyes about what you are gaining and what you are giving up. The gap between hedged and unhedged operators within each sector remains the key competitive variable during price shocks, but hedging decisions should be informed by firm-specific exposure analysis, not by fear alone.

"The critical point is this: the businesses that hedged before the crisis are now insulated, temporarily, from the worst of the shock. Those that didn't are passing costs directly to consumers, or absorbing losses that threaten their viability. And even the best hedges eventually roll off. If oil remains above $100 for six months, twelve months, or longer, even well-hedged companies will be repricing at the new reality. The futures markets don't eliminate risk, they redistribute it across time and across participants. In a sustained crisis, they buy time. But they cannot repeal the fundamental economics of a world that runs on oil."
